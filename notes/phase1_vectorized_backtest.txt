  PHASE 1 — VECTORIZED BACKTEST
  What we built, why we built it this way, and what to notice

WHAT IS PHASE 1?
────────────────
Phase 0 gave us clean, reproducible price data. Phase 1 asks:
    "Given that data, what would have happened if we followed a rule?"

We choose the simplest possible rule: a moving-average crossover.
We run it across the full price history in one shot (vectorized).
We measure realistic performance metrics including transaction costs.

The "done" condition:
    Sane numbers you can explain on a whiteboard — and that become
    the ground truth for Phase 2 (the event-driven version must
    reproduce these exactly).


THE FILES WE CREATED
─────────────────────

strategy/ma_crossover.py
    generate_signals(bars, fast, slow) → pd.Series

    Takes a price DataFrame and two window sizes. Returns a signal:
    1.0 = go long, 0.0 = stay flat, NaN = not enough history yet.

    The rule: buy when the 50-day average is above the 200-day average.
    Exit when it crosses back below.

backtest/vectorized.py
    run(bars, signals, fee_bps) → pd.DataFrame

    The core backtesting logic. Applies the signal to the price data,
    accounts for transaction costs, and returns a result DataFrame
    with columns: position, market_return, gross_return, fee,
    net_return, equity.

backtest/metrics.py
    compute_metrics(returns, positions) → dict

    Computes the six standard performance metrics from a returns series.
    Implemented from scratch so every formula is visible and testable.

tests/test_vectorized.py and tests/test_metrics.py
    Unit tests for both modules, including the look-ahead bias check.

config.yaml (updated)
    Added a `backtest:` section with fast_ma, slow_ma, fee_bps,
    and train_ratio — all configurable without touching Python code.


THE ALGORITHM, STEP BY STEP
────────────────────────────

1. LOAD DATA
   bars = get_bars("SPY", "2010-01-01", "2024-12-31")
   → ~3,521 rows of daily OHLCV

2. GENERATE SIGNALS
   fast_ma[t] = mean(close[t-49:t+1])   (50-day)
   slow_ma[t] = mean(close[t-199:t+1])  (200-day)
   signal[t]  = 1.0  if fast_ma[t] > slow_ma[t]
              = 0.0  otherwise
              = NaN  if t < 199  (warmup — not enough history)

   The first 199 bars have NaN signals and are discarded before backtesting.

3. WALK-FORWARD SPLIT (train_ratio=0.70)
   in-sample  = first 70% of bars  (~2010–2020)
   out-of-sample = last 30% of bars (~2020–2024)

   We report metrics on BOTH, but the out-of-sample (OOS) number is the
   honest one. In-sample numbers are always optimistic — the strategy
   "saw" that data implicitly when we chose fast=50, slow=200.

4. PREVENT LOOK-AHEAD BIAS
   position[t] = signal[t-1]    ← THE CRITICAL LINE

   We cannot act on today's closing price before today's close happens.
   Shifting the signal by one bar means: the position for bar T was
   decided at the close of bar T-1. This costs exactly one bar of lag
   but prevents a form of cheating that is extremely easy to miss.

5. COMPUTE RETURNS
   market_return[t] = (close[t] - close[t-1]) / close[t-1]
   gross_return[t]  = position[t] × market_return[t]

   Example: position=1 on a day that goes +1.5% → gross_return=+1.5%
            position=0 on a day that goes +1.5% → gross_return=0%

6. APPLY FEES
   fee_rate = fee_bps / 10_000     (10 bps → 0.0010)
   trade[t] = |position[t] - position[t-1]|
   fee[t]   = trade[t] × fee_rate

   trade[t] = 1 when we enter or exit a full position, 0 when we hold.
   So we pay fee_rate once to buy in and once to sell out.

   10 bps round-trip (20 bps total) is realistic for a liquid ETF.
   For crypto spot trading with taker fees of ~10 bps, this is optimistic.

7. NET RETURN
   net_return[t] = gross_return[t] - fee[t]

8. EQUITY CURVE
   equity[0] = 1.0
   equity[t] = equity[t-1] × (1 + net_return[t])
             = product(1 + net_return[0..t])

9. METRICS
   total_return = equity[-1] - 1

   years        = N / 252
   ann_return   = (1 + total_return)^(1/years) - 1

   ann_vol      = std(net_return, ddof=1) × √252

   sharpe       = ann_return / ann_vol         (rf = 0)

   For each t: drawdown[t] = equity[t] / max(equity[0..t]) - 1
   max_drawdown = min(drawdown)

   daily_turnover = mean(|position[t] - position[t-1]|)
   ann_turnover   = daily_turnover × 252


KEY CONCEPTS TO UNDERSTAND
───────────────────────────

1. WHY VECTORIZED FIRST?

   Vectorized = apply numpy/pandas operations to the full array at once.
   It's fast, readable, and easy to reason about for research.

   The downside: nothing structurally prevents you from accidentally
   using future data. A single missing .shift(1) gives you perfect
   foresight and a fantasy backtest. Vectorized code requires
   discipline; the event-driven engine (Phase 2) removes the risk.

2. WHY THIS STRATEGY?

   The 50/200-day "golden cross" is possibly the most-cited trend rule
   in existence. It's not a secret edge — it's a well-known baseline.

   That's the point. For a developer portfolio, the signal is irrelevant.
   What matters is that the system around it is correct. An interviewer
   who sees you understand look-ahead bias, walk-forward splits, and
   realistic fee modeling will not care that your strategy is simple.

3. WHY SHIFT(1)?

   Consider what happens without the shift:
   - signal[t] is computed from close[t]
   - position[t] = signal[t]   (no lag)
   - return[t] = position[t] × (close[t] / close[t-1] - 1)

   This means: on bar t, we look at today's close, decide to go long,
   AND get paid for the price move into today's close — all
   simultaneously. We're trading at a price we already know.

   Real execution happens AFTER the close. By shifting: we decide
   at close of t-1 and execute at close of t. That's at minimum a
   one-bar delay, which is conservative (execution is usually at the
   open of t, not close).

4. WHY 10 BPS?

   One basis point = 0.01%. Ten basis points = 0.10%.

   For SPY on a US equity exchange, actual taker fees are typically
   0–3 bps depending on venue and tier. 10 bps is conservative and
   deliberately so — it includes a rough spread crossing.

   For crypto spot, typical taker fees are 5–10 bps. Our 10 bps is
   roughly right but ignores slippage (price impact of the order),
   which matters more for larger positions and thinner markets.

5. THE WALK-FORWARD DISCIPLINE

   Quant finance term: "in-sample" is data your strategy parameters
   were chosen on; "out-of-sample" is data it has never seen.

   Even though we didn't optimize fast=50 and slow=200 (they're
   conventional values), reporting only the in-sample result is
   misleading. We always split:
   - IN-SAMPLE: used for understanding and iteration.
   - OOS: the honest number. What you'd report in an interview.

   The danger of not splitting: you tune your strategy on 2010–2024,
   report a Sharpe of 1.2, publish the "strategy." It promptly earns
   0.0 going forward because you overfit to history.

6. ANNUALIZATION CONVENTIONS

   We use 252 trading days per year throughout:
   - ann_vol   = daily_vol × √252
   - ann_return = (1 + total_return)^(252/N) - 1
   - ann_turnover = daily_turnover × 252

   Using 365 would slightly underestimate annualized figures
   (√365 > √252, so ann_vol would be 20% larger). Quant convention
   is always 252 for daily equity/futures series.


WHAT TO NOTICE IN THE OUTPUT
──────────────────────────────
When you run `python main.py`, look for:

1. The in-sample Sharpe will typically be higher than OOS.
   This is normal — it's not cheating, just the signal having
   worked better historically than recently.

2. The OOS Sharpe vs buy-and-hold Sharpe:
   The 50/200 MA crossover often trails passive buy-and-hold in
   strong bull markets because it exits and misses recoveries.

3. Turnover of approximately 1–4x per year.
   With 10 bps fees and ~2x turnover, annual fee drag is:
       2 × 2 × 10 bps = 40 bps per year
   Compared to 10–15% annual returns, this is relatively small.
   But for a high-frequency strategy with 252x turnover and the same
   fee, the drag is 252 × 2 × 10 bps = 50.4% per year — lethal.


QUESTIONS TO ASK YOURSELF
───────────────────────────
Before moving to Phase 2, make sure you can answer these:

1. What would happen to the Sharpe ratio if you set fee_bps=100?
   At what fee level does the strategy become net-negative?

2. What does it mean if in-sample Sharpe is 1.5 but OOS is 0.2?
   Is that a problem with the strategy or the backtest?

3. We use close-to-close returns. What look-ahead does that still
   contain, even with shift(1)? (Hint: when does the close happen
   relative to when you can place an order?)

4. The equity curve is a product of (1 + net_return). Why
   cumulative product and not cumulative sum? What would summing
   the returns mean in practice?


WHAT'S NEXT — PHASE 2
──────────────────────
Phase 2 replaces this vectorized loop with an event-driven engine.

The event loop processes one bar at a time, in chronological order,
and the strategy reacts to each event without the ability to look ahead.

The critical test: Phase 2 must produce the SAME metrics as Phase 1
(within floating-point tolerance). If it doesn't, there's a bug in
either the event loop or the fee/position accounting — and finding it
teaches you exactly how accounting errors hide in real systems.
